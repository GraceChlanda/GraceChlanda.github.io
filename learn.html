Overview

There are a lot of misconceptions about Service Dog laws due to a large number of online scams. The Department of Justice (DOJ), the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), and the Housing and Urban Development act (HUD), work together to outline the laws for Service Dogs (SDs) and their handlers. These laws allow a SD to accompany a disabled individual into public domains with the understanding that the SD is trained to preform a task that mitigates the handler's disability.

Emotional Support Animals (ESAs) are different than SDs because they require a prescription and don't need training. While SDs need to be specifically trained, the law doesn't require that they be professionally trained. This is largely due to the lack of regulation in the dog training industry. Because of this, if a dog can preform their mitigating task, is bathroom trained, doesn't bark more than once, and is under control, then they qualify as an SD.

To qualify as a SD handler, one must have a disability. The ADA defines this disability separately from Social Security. The ADA considers any record of an impairment that substantially limits a major life activity to be a disability. Handlers don't need to disclose their disability to anyone except, doctors, businesses they are requesting work accommodations from, and landlords.

Similarly, there is no SD registry and SDs don't need to wear anything to identify them. Businesses can protect themselves from frauds by asking if the dog is a service dog and which tasks their dog preforms to help it. Housing and workplaces can go beyond this by requiring medical proof of disability and assessing the need for reasonable accommodation.

Training Principles

Overview

To be a successful dog trainer, there are some concepts that need to be understood. These include Classical Conditioning, Operant Conditioning, Counter Conditioning, the Premack Principle, Chaining Behaviors, Lumping, Primary vs. Secondary Reinforcers, Generalization, Shaping, Extinction, Behavior Modification, Behavior Management, Positive Interrupters, and Schedules of Reinforcement.

All of these concepts are already built into common training practices. Understanding these, however, will give you a better idea of how to personalize your training plan to your specific dog. The summary of all of these concepts, is that you must ask your dog to do simple things in a variety of circumstances while rewarding the behavior you want to see. If your dog is having a hard time learning, it is likely because of one of these three things:

  • you're asking something too complex
  • the environment is overwhelming
  • the reinforcer is not strong, Immediate, or consistent enough

Foundational Training

Developmental and Training Timeline

Socialization

To understand foundational training, you need to look at the developmental stages of a dog. For example, puppies don't gain control of their bladder until around 12-16 weeks, so trying to potty train them earlier than that would be counterproductive.

One of the most important parts of service dog training is socialization. This starts from birth all the way up until the age of 16 weeks. You can still do socialization training after this, but it will be harder. For this training, the goal is to cultivate a calm and positive reaction from the dog to their environment. This allows your service dog in training (SDIT) to focus on working instead of being too excited or fearful.

It's important to socialize your SDIT to things like elevators, bikes, wheelchairs, store displays, loudspeakers, and children through conditioning and counterconditioning. There are many places online with example socialization lists such as this one:

What to Train and When?

While you do want to make sure your SDIT is comfortable with everything, it’s important not to overwork them. Puppies reach Adolescence at 5 to 6 months, and it continues until they are 18 to 24 months old. During this time their attention spans shrink, and they often get frustrated easily due to their hormones. So, it is best to take it easy during this time and keep things fun so that your SDIT is still motivated to work for you.

So how much should you train? Based on personal experience and the advice of other SD trainers, puppies learn best in 10-minute sessions 2 times a day. Adolescents learn best in 5-minute sessions 3 or 4 times a day. Adults learn best in 15-minute sessions 1 to 2 times a day.

Task Training

How to Choose Your Tasks

Task training is the very last thing that needs to be trained, and it’s what takes your dog from being a well-trained pet to a service dog.

The disability you have will determine the task that your dog will do to help mitigate your disability. For example, I have flashbacks and intrusive thoughts that trigger vocal and physical tics. So, one of the tasks my SD is trained to do is provide tactile stimulation by jumping on me and scratching me when she notices this happening. By doing this she helps distract me from my thoughts so that I can refocus on whatever it was that I was doing.

Some other example tasks include providing something to brace against for people with balance and mobility issues; retrieving things the handler cannot; checking things for allergens; smelling changes in blood glucose; leading a handler away from unsafe situations; and alerting a handler to changes in heart rate.

Once you get to this part of your training you should be able to use all the training principles and foundations to train whichever behavior you will help you.
